Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing in Motion

Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (OFDM) tames frequency-selective fading and inter-symbol interference by slicing a wideband channel into many narrow orthogonal subcarriers. Each subcarrier sees nearly flat fading, so equalization collapses to one complex coefficient per tone. Adding a cyclic prefix turns linear convolution into a cyclic one, keeping symbols orthogonal after the FFT at the receiver.

From 802.11a theory to an interactive symbol

IEEE 802.11a pours a 20 MHz channel into 64 orthogonal subcarriers. Forty-eight carry user data, four provide pilot tones, and the rest form guard bands with a nulled DC tone. The useful symbol lasts 3.2 µs; appending a 0.8 µs cyclic prefix pushes the air time to 4 µs while absorbing multipath echoes.

Use the controls to adjust the cyclic prefix length and the channel's delay spread. When the prefix outpaces the delay spread, the system retains orthogonality and equalization stays simple. If the channel memory spills past the prefix, adjacent OFDM symbols leak into one another.

Cyclic prefix shorter than delay spread → residual ISI likely.
